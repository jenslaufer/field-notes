# 9 Huge Startup Opportunities in the AI Boom

> Greg and Jonathan pressure-test build-now niches across B2C, AI, mobile, and
> IRL (from Greg's viral "30 startup opportunities" tweet — each picks his
> favorites live). The meta-point: AI makes building cheap, so the edge moves to
> *distribution and audience* — pick a niche worth marrying, not a feature worth
> shipping.

## The three biggest bets (Greg's own ranking)

- **Biggest B2C: loneliness.** Third spaces, community apps, IRL hangouts.
- **Biggest B2B: managed AI employees** sold to small businesses.
- **Biggest overlooked: elder tech.** ~70M US boomers with money and unmet needs.

## The 9 opportunities

### 1. Live, unscripted creator shows (Twitch for business)
The polished interview podcast is losing to long, messy, live streams where the
host just talks. **Concrete examples Jonathan gave:**
- **Jeff Gerstmann** — a streamer Jonathan *pays* to watch; does 3–4 streams a
  week, 3–4 hours each, unedited, just talking to chat.
- **Druski** streaming the game *Marathon* — ~247 people watching live, many
  paying, while he plays a game he'd play anyway.
- **Giant Bomb** — streams every weekday; closer to the "business show" format.
- **TBPN** (the X tech show) **sold for >$100M** — low viewership but very
  *high-value* viewership. The lesson: which other high-value audiences need a
  live show?
- Counter-example of the dying format: **The Diary of a CEO** — hyper-polished,
  interview-driven.

**The money math (from Jonathan's own bet):** with only **1,000–2,000 viewers**
every Tuesday he says he can make **€400,000–500,000/year** with *no ads* — just
in-person events and merch. Public **Patreon** numbers show some shows pulling
**$70,000–100,000/month**. You don't need a huge audience. As AI sanitizes the
feed, **anti-AI authenticity** (live, can-go-wrong, human) keeps gaining share.

### 2. Action apps (agent-first mobile)
Greg's favorite category ("100+ ideas in it"). Today's apps assume a human taps,
scrolls, types. The **agent-first** version *does the work and shows you only the
exceptions*. **Concrete examples:**
- **Superhuman** — sold as an AI inbox, but you still do everything manually.
- **Incogni** — a *set-and-forget* background privacy app (removes you from spam
  lists). Sparks Jonathan's open question: when a product is so automated you
  never touch it, does retention suffer? (You forget you're paying.)
- The email reframe: an app that **answers ~80% of your emails via agents** and
  surfaces only the 1–2 per day too important to auto-handle.

The pattern repeats web→mobile (Facebook lagged → bought Instagram); incumbents
will lag agent-first the same way, so the play is to rebuild a known tool
agent-native and get acquired. **You don't build the AI from scratch** — wrap the
**Claude Agent SDK** ("GPT wrappers" → "agent wrappers"). Winning formula:
mobile-first + agent + niche + willingness-to-spend + boring workflow + a
TikTok/Reel hook to drive installs.

### 3. Loneliness, third spaces, niche communities
The biggest B2C bet. **Concrete examples:**
- **Dads of Marathon** — a Discord for casual/dad gamers of one niche game,
  **13,898 members** (Jonathan: nearly more people in the group than actively
  playing the game). His main online community for 3 months; first online friends
  in years.
- **Jonathan's 5-day off-grid retreat (February)** — no phones/tech, summer-camp
  style. **Cost ~$90K, made ~$90K (broke even)** — run so attendees could build
  their own versions.
- **222** (Greg is a small investor) — take a personality test → pick an
  experience (dinners, cocktail bars, salsa nights, basketball) → meet 5–7
  matched people IRL in your city. "Crushing it."
- **Fabric** — **75+ gatherings/month**, NYC + Chicago, **~500 members**, long
  waitlist, **5,000–10,000 sq ft** spaces (repurposed empty commercial real
  estate into community infrastructure).

**The stat:** ~**22% of Americans have fewer than one close friend.** The play:
paid **memberships** (recurring revenue), niche Discords, physical spaces.
Productization: "**Dads play [game/hobby/niche]**" → the dad company. Best fit for
a founder who actually *likes* organizing events.

### 4. Elder tech for 65+
The biggest *overlooked* bet. **~70M US boomers**, disposable income, underserved
pain: hearing, mobility, social, memory, vision. **Concrete example:** Jonathan's
**Facilitator.com** assumed a 20-something tech audience — but once they ran
**broad Facebook ads** (no tight targeting), their **highest-spending customers
turned out to be 45+** (highest earners / most savings). "Fish where the fish
are." Counterintuitive tactic: **Facebook ads still work** — just for the 50–60+
crowd, not Gen Z. Design note: don't make the landing page look like a retirement
home — older users don't see themselves that way. Future tailwind: gamers are
aging ("**grandpas of Marathon**" in 10 years).

### 5. Adult hobbies
Adults pour money and identity into hobbies (pottery, woodworking, drawing, music,
gardening). **Concrete examples:**
- **Jonathan's painting/art retreat (February)** — sold out essentially instantly,
  made **$90K–110K**. Other creative retreats "usually make ~$5K" — the
  difference was **targeting entrepreneurs**, not the general public (see #stacking).
- **Dr. Tyler Lemco's Creative Club** — a comedian friend's space hosting events
  (e.g. a **paper-lamp workshop**) for people who want to make something instead
  of staring at a phone; people go on first dates there. Charges for events,
  doing well.
- A **paid Berlin book club** — **~85 people every Sunday**, paying, just to be
  around other humans.

Low competition *because* most people find it "boring" to run. Audience
self-selects and spends.

### 6. AI employees & AI agents
The biggest B2B bet (Greg has a whole prior episode: "how to sell **$5,000/month
AI employees**" with his friend Nick). **Recipe:** pick a **vertical** → pick a
**job title** → ask Claude/ChatGPT to list the **50 jobs-to-be-done** → ship an
agent that does them for **~1/10 of a junior's salary**, then keep adding
jobs-to-be-done until it's a true digital employee. **Concrete example:** a
**junior YouTube producer** — auto-add chapters to video + audio, generate clips
to choose from, prep thumbnails (Jonathan's real unmet need; tools like **Opus
Clip** exist but still require too much manual glue). Key insight: founders don't
*know* the use cases — winners make the use case **crystal clear**. Branding move:
sell a **"Juniors" / junioremployees.ai** ("junior, not senior replacement") to
clear the trust bar — and optionally position as **anti-AI for creative work,
pro-AI for menial tasks** to appeal to creatives.

### 7. Personalized nutrition/health by vertical
**Concrete examples:**
- Jonathan pays for **Aware** (Berlin blood-testing service) — "a lot of money for
  very little"; exports the data into a **Claude project he named "Stomach
  Helper"** to actually make sense of it (he has **GERD**).
- **Zoe** — microbiome DNA test kit → app → personalized evidence-based nutrition
  (a "**23andMe meets Cal AI**" model). **Cal AI** (AI food tracking) does **$50M
  ARR**. **Function Health** — people pay hundreds–thousands/year for health
  markers.

**The stat:** ~**60M Americans (~20% of adults) have GERD** — that's why CVS has
whole aisles of antacids. **Heuristic to find verticals:** walk into a CVS and
see what over-the-counter stuff fills the most shelf space (GERD, migraines, …).
The play: Zoe-but-vertical (one condition, or "Zoe for men / for Germany"),
ideally extending to **sending the food / certified chefs**, not generic wellness.
Maxim: "**date the product, marry the niche.**"

### 8. Pet health & AI for animals
Owners now treat pets like family ("their baby"), but tooling is thin. **Pet
industry ~$140–150B; <2% of pets have smart monitoring.** **Concrete examples:**
- **PetPace** (Jonathan's current collar) and **Whistle** — collars tracking
  heart rate, step count, sleep; a few have *just* added AI. Still very early.
- **Kevin Rose** (on the Tim Ferriss podcast) discusses **dog longevity** —
  resveratrol and other supplements.
- Greg once suggested "**Athletic Greens for pets / Eight Sleep for pets**" on My
  First Million — **someone built it and reportedly makes hundreds of thousands a
  month.**

**Heuristics:** take any human-health play and point it at animals; or look at
**who sponsors the Huberman podcast** and build the pet version. Add a "$30 AI
brain" to existing pet hardware. High emotional willingness-to-pay.

### 9. AI-native media companies done right
At the **top-1% quality bar**, not slop. **Concrete example:** **Rowan Cheung**
grew an account from **0 → ~400K followers in 18–24 months** using an **AI avatar**
covering news — then can build and sell products to that audience. Tooling:
**HeyGen** + **ElevenLabs**, ideally human-in-the-loop. Slop loses in 2026; media
becomes the **distribution arm** of a real business (e.g. a faceless,
research-excellent GERD channel that funds a real expert + live streams).

## Stacking the ideas
Jonathan's explicit playbook: combine **#1 live unscripted show → #3/#5 IRL
retreats → a community of entrepreneurs**. The hidden lever is **who you target** —
his art retreat made **$90–110K vs. the typical ~$5K** purely because the audience
was **entrepreneurs** (disposable income, established), funneled from his show.
Free meetups (lamp-making, low-cost) at the bottom, **$7,000–8,000 premium
retreats** at the top (Tim Ferriss "free version / premium version"). A recent
**virtual** event: **$60K for 3 hours/day over 2 days.**

## Throughline
AI collapses the cost of building, so the moat shifts to **audience, trust, and
distribution**. Every winning idea pairs an underserved niche with a channel the
founder can actually own (Discord, Facebook ads, live streams, owned media). Pick
a niche that is (1) underserved, (2) has disposable income, (3) has a real pain
point. **Date the product, marry the niche.**
